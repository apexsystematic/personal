Sleep Transition Guide · Dr. Mia

Kai's Plan for Better Nights

For Jorge & Kai's mom · Kai is 18 months old
First: Kai is not being harmed.

Kai currently sleeps 12–14 hours per day total. That meets every international guideline for his age. (AAP/WHO joint guidelines) His brain is getting what it needs.

The goal of this plan is not to fix something broken — it's to help him learn to connect his own sleep cycles without needing the breast. He is completely capable of this. He already does it four nights a week with Jorge.

Research consistently shows that sleep transitions done with a present, responsive caregiver cause no harm to attachment, emotional development, or neurological health. (Gradisar et al., Pediatrics, 2016; Price et al., Pediatrics, 2012)

The Science

Why Kai wakes up at night

All humans — adults included — surface briefly between sleep cycles every 1–3 hours. We don't remember it because we've learned to drift back down on our own. Kai hasn't learned that yet, because nursing has always been the bridge. Every time he surfaces, his brain says: "I need that to get back to sleep." (Mindell et al., Pediatrics, 2006)

This is not a flaw. It's learned behaviour. And it can be unlearned — gently, with consistency, and with Jorge as his safe person through the transition.

Before Night One

What needs to be in place first

  • Mom commits to sleeping in another room for a minimum of 2 weeks — no exceptions, not even one night
  • Both parents agree on a start date and a clear 2-week minimum before assessing progress
  • Mom has a plan to manage engorgement — pump as needed for comfort
  • Pick a week without big stressors — travel, illness, major changes
  • Both parents understand: hearing Kai cry is not harm. Protest is expected and temporary.
The Plan

Night by night approach

Nights 1–3

The hardest nights — expect them

  • Jorge does bedtime as normal
  • When Kai wakes, go to him immediately — presence, patting, calm voice
  • Keep interactions brief and boring — you're there, but sleep is the agenda
  • Expect protest. Sit with it. Your calm is his signal that everything is safe.
Nights 4–7

The shift begins

  • Wakings should start reducing in frequency
  • Same response every time — consistency is everything
  • If he stands and cries, sit next to him or briefly pick up then put down — whichever you can do consistently
Week 2

Consolidation

  • Most children show clear improvement by now
  • Begin gradually reducing your intervention — less patting, more just presence
  • He is learning to bridge his own sleep cycles
Non-negotiables

Rules that make or break this

  • Mom does not come in. Not once. Even briefly. Kai can smell her and it resets everything.
  • No nursing at night. Under any circumstances, from either parent.
  • Consistency beats technique. The method matters less than doing the same thing every single time. (Mindell et al., Pediatrics, 2006)
  • Two weeks minimum before judging progress. Night 3 is not representative. Night 10 is.
  • If Kai is unwell, pause. Never sleep train through illness or major developmental stress.
A note for Kai's mom

What you'll feel — and why it doesn't mean stop

You will hear Kai cry and every instinct will say go to him. That instinct is good mothering, not a reason to abandon the plan.

Staying away is the loving act here. You are giving him the chance to learn one of the most important skills of early childhood: how to self-settle. You are not abandoning him. Jorge is there. Kai is safe.

Kai has a secure attachment to you — research shows securely attached children handle these transitions well and show no lasting distress. (Ainsworth & Bell, Child Development, 1970; Gradisar et al., Pediatrics, 2016)

The separation is temporary. The skill he gains is permanent.
